graf--h3 graf--leading graf--title">What is a quantum computer? Explained with a simple example.</h1><figure name="ae58" id="ae58" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--h3"><div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 700px; max-height: 466px;"><div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder-fill" style="padding-bottom: 66.60000000000001%;"></div><div class="progressiveMedia js-progressiveMedia graf-image" data-image-id="0*juPpwvGEsj0xky-d" data-width="2275" data-height="1516" data-is-featured="true" data-action="zoom" data-action-value="0*juPpwvGEsj0xky-d"><img src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/freeze/max/60/0*juPpwvGEsj0xky-d?q=20" crossorigin="anonymous" class="progressiveMedia-thumbnail js-progressiveMedia-thumbnail"><canvas class="progressiveMedia-canvas js-progressiveMedia-canvas"></canvas><img class="progressiveMedia-image js-progressiveMedia-image" data-src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/0*juPpwvGEsj0xky-d"><noscript class="js-progressiveMedia-inner"><img class="progressiveMedia-noscript js-progressiveMedia-inner" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/0*juPpwvGEsj0xky-d"></noscript></div></div></figure><p name="99cd" id="99cd" class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure">Hi everyone!</p><p name="5583" id="5583" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The other day, I visited <a href="https://www.dwavesys.com/" data-href="https://www.dwavesys.com/" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">D-Wave Systems</strong></a> in Vancouver, Canada. It’s a company that makes cutting-edge quantum computers.</p><p name="0473" id="0473" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">I got to learn a lot about quantum computers there, so I’d like to share some of what I learned there with you in this article.</p><p name="0bf2" id="0bf2" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The goal of this article is to give you an accurate intuition of what a quantum computer is using a simple example.</p><p name="9446" id="9446" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This article will not require you to have prior knowledge of either quantum physics or computer science to be able to understand it.</p><p name="e6b5" id="e6b5" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Okay, let’s get started.</p><h3 name="08b3" id="08b3" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">What is a quantum computer?</h3><p name="1932" id="1932" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Here is a one-sentence summary of what a quantum computer is:</p><blockquote name="acc0" id="acc0" class="graf graf--blockquote graf-after--p">A quantum computer is a type of computer that uses quantum mechanics so that it can perform certain kinds of computation more efficiently than a regular computer can.</blockquote><p name="5c3a" id="5c3a" class="graf graf--p graf-after--blockquote">There is a lot to unpack in this sentence, so let me walk you through what it is exactly using a simple example.</p><p name="fa8f" id="fa8f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">To explain what a quantum computer is, I’ll need to first explain a little bit about regular (non-quantum) computers.</p><h3 name="353d" id="353d" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">How a regular computer stores information</h3><p name="5862" id="5862" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Now, a regular computer stores information in a series of 0’s and 1’s.</p><p name="8ccf" id="8ccf" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Different kinds of information, such as numbers, text, and images can be represented this way.</p><p name="d22b" id="d22b" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Each unit in this series of 0’s and 1’s is called a bit. So, a bit can be set to either 0 or 1.</p><h4 name="dd38" id="dd38" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--p">Now, what about quantum computers?</h4><p name="df67" id="df67" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">A quantum computer <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">does not</strong> use bits to store information. Instead, it uses something called qubits.</p><p name="8b24" id="8b24" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Each qubit can not only be set to 1 <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">or</strong> 0, but it can also be set to 1 <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">and</strong> 0. But what does that mean exactly?</p><p name="8df1" id="8df1" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Let me explain this with a simple example. This is going to be a somewhat artificial example. But it’s still going to be helpful in understanding how quantum computers work.</p><h3 name="be58" id="be58" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--h3-strong">A simple example for understanding how quantum computers work</strong></h3><p name="88d9" id="88d9" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Now, suppose you’re running a travel agency, and you need to move a group of people from one location to another.</p><p name="2337" id="2337" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">To keep this simple, let’s say that you need to move only 3 people for now — Alice, Becky, and Chris.</p><p name="9f4d" id="9f4d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">And suppose that you have booked 2 taxis for this purpose, and you want to figure out who gets into which taxi.</p><p name="8c49" id="8c49" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Also, suppose here that you’re given information about who’s friends with who, and who’s enemies with who.</p><p name="10c6" id="10c6" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Here, let’s say that:</p><ul class="postList"><li name="1cd3" id="1cd3" class="graf graf--li graf-after--p">Alice and Becky are friends</li><li name="ae38" id="ae38" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">Alice and Chris are enemies</li><li name="0eff" id="0eff" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">Becky and Chris are enemies</li></ul><p name="9a02" id="9a02" class="graf graf--p graf-after--li">And suppose that your goal here is to divide this group of 3 people into the two taxis to achieve the following two objectives:</p><ul class="postList"><li name="a21e" id="a21e" class="graf graf--li graf-after--p">Maximize the number of <strong class="markup--strong markup--li-strong">friend pairs </strong>that share the same car</li><li name="8c9e" id="8c9e" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">Minimize the number of <strong class="markup--strong markup--li-strong">enemy pairs </strong>that share the same car</li></ul><p name="a5e2" id="a5e2" class="graf graf--p graf-after--li">Okay, so this is the basic premise of this problem. Let’s first think about how we would solve this problem using a regular computer.</p><h4 name="26a7" id="26a7" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--h4-strong">Solving this problem with a regular computer</strong></h4><p name="f053" id="f053" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">To solve this problem with a regular, non-quantum computer, you’ll need first to figure out how to store the relevant information with bits.</p><p name="b67f" id="b67f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Let’s label the two taxis Taxi #1 and Taxi #0.</p><p name="8cf7" id="8cf7" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Then, you can represent who gets into which car with 3 bits.</p><p name="afda" id="afda" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">For example, we can set the three bits to <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">0</strong>, <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">0</strong>,<em class="markup--em markup--p-em"> </em>and <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">1</strong> to represent:</p><ul class="postList"><li name="c0cd" id="c0cd" class="graf graf--li graf-after--p">Alice gets into Taxi #0</li><li name="0800" id="0800" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">Becky gets into Taxi #0</li><li name="51bf" id="51bf" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">Chris gets into Taxi #1</li></ul><p name="87b8" id="87b8" class="graf graf--p graf-after--li">Since there are two choices for each person, there are 2*2*2 = 8 ways to divide this group of people into two cars.</p><p name="4af8" id="4af8" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Here’s a list of all possible configurations:</p><p name="f562" id="f562" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">A | B | C<br>0 | 0 | 0<br>0 | 0 | 1<br>0 | 1 | 0<br>0 | 1 | 1<br>1 | 0 | 0<br>1 | 0 | 1<br>1 | 1 | 0<br>1 | 1 | 1</p><p name="e772" id="e772" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Using 3 bits, you can represent any one of these combinations.</p><h4 name="6e94" id="6e94" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--p">Computing the score for each configuration</h4><p name="813b" id="813b" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">Now, using a regular computer, how would we determine which configuration is the best solution?</p><p name="038b" id="038b" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">To do this, let’s define how we can compute the score for each configuration. This score will represent the extent to which each solution achieves the two objectives I mentioned earlier:</p><ul class="postList"><li name="b316" id="b316" class="graf graf--li graf-after--p">Maximize the number of <strong class="markup--strong markup--li-strong">friend pairs </strong>that share the same car</li><li name="c027" id="c027" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">Minimize the number of <strong class="markup--strong markup--li-strong">enemy pairs </strong>that share the same car</li></ul><p name="2396" id="2396" class="graf graf--p graf-after--li">Let’s simply define our score as follows:</p><p name="f704" id="f704" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">(the score of a given configuration) = (# friend pairs sharing the same car) - (# enemy pairs sharing the same car)</p><p name="179f" id="179f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">For example, suppose that Alice, Becky, and Chris all get into Taxi #1. With three bits, this can be expressed as <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">111</strong>.</p><p name="9bc4" id="9bc4" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">In this case, there is only <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">one friend pair</strong> sharing the same car — Alice and Becky.</p><p name="391a" id="391a" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">However, there are <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">two enemy pairs</strong> sharing the same car — Alice and Chris, and Becky and Chris.</p><p name="22cd" id="22cd" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">So, the total score of this configuration is 1-2 = -1.</p><h4 name="a94a" id="a94a" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--p">Solving the problem</h4><p name="3c88" id="3c88" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">With all of this setup, we can finally go about solving this problem.</p><p name="4c85" id="4c85" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">With a regular computer, to find the best configuration, you’ll need to essentially go through all configurations to see which one achieves the highest score.</p><p name="d58f" id="d58f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">So, you can think about constructing a table like this:</p><p name="6006" id="6006" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">A | B | C | Score<br>0 | 0 | 0 | -1<br>0 | 0 | 1 | 1 &lt;- one of the best solutions<br>0 | 1 | 0 | -1<br>0 | 1 | 1 | -1<br>1 | 0 | 0 | -1<br>1 | 0 | 1 | -1<br>1 | 1 | 0 | 1 &lt;- the other best solution<br>1 | 1 | 1 | -1</p><p name="043d" id="043d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">As you can see, there are two correct solutions here — 001 and 110, both achieving the score of 1.</p><p name="65be" id="65be" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This problem is fairly simple. It quickly becomes too difficult to solve with a regular computer as we increase the number of people in this problem.</p><p name="b423" id="b423" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">We saw that with 3 people, we need to go through 8 possible configurations.</p><p name="47d6" id="47d6" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">What if there are 4 people? In that case, we’ll need to go through 2*2*2*2 = 16 configurations.</p><p name="fc93" id="fc93" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">With n people, we’ll need to go through (2 to the power of n) configurations to find the best solution.</p><p name="b5d9" id="b5d9" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">So, if there are 100 people, we’ll need to go through:</p><ul class="postList"><li name="c307" id="c307" class="graf graf--li graf-after--p">2¹⁰⁰ ~= 10³⁰ = one million million million million million configurations.</li></ul><p name="1083" id="1083" class="graf graf--p graf-after--li">This is simply impossible to solve with a regular computer.</p><h4 name="f603" id="f603" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--p">Solving this problem with a quantum computer</h4><p name="afca" id="afca" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">How would we go about solving this problem with a quantum computer?</p><p name="c7c1" id="c7c1" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">To think about that, let’s go back to the case of dividing 3 people into two taxis.</p><p name="730e" id="730e" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">As we saw earlier, there were 8 possible solutions to this problem:</p><p name="ec52" id="ec52" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">A | B | C<br>0 | 0 | 0<br>0 | 0 | 1<br>0 | 1 | 0<br>0 | 1 | 1<br>1 | 0 | 0<br>1 | 0 | 1<br>1 | 1 | 0<br>1 | 1 | 1</p><p name="17be" id="17be" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">With a regular computer, using 3 bits, we were able to represent only one of these solutions at a time — for example, 001.</p><p name="d3b3" id="d3b3" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">However, with a quantum computer, using 3 <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">qubits</strong>, we can represent <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">all 8 of these solutions at the same time</strong>.</p><p name="5139" id="5139" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">There are debates as to what it means exactly, but here’s the way I think about it.</p><p name="201a" id="201a" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">First, examine the first qubit out of these 3 qubits. When you set it to <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">both</strong><em class="markup--em markup--p-em"> </em>0 and 1, it’s sort of like creating two parallel worlds. (Yes, it’s strange, but just follow along here.)</p><p name="a832" id="a832" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">In one of those parallel worlds, the qubit is set to 0. In the other one, it’s set to 1.</p><p name="bbbf" id="bbbf" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Now, what if you set the second qubit to 0 <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">and</strong> 1, too? Then, it’s sort of like creating 4 parallel worlds.</p><p name="229b" id="229b" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">In the first world, the two qubits are set to 00. In the second one, they are 01. In the third one, they are 10. In the fourth one, they are 11.</p><p name="ca0f" id="ca0f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Similarly, if you set all three qubits to both 0 and 1, you’d be creating 8 parallel worlds — 000, 001, 010, 011, 100, 101, 110, and 111.</p><p name="fe4c" id="fe4c" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This is a strange way to think, but it is one of the correct ways to interpret how the qubits behave in the real world.</p><p name="2ca5" id="2ca5" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Now, when you apply some sort of computation on these three qubits, you are actually applying the same computation in all of those 8 parallel worlds at the same time.</p><p name="a42b" id="a42b" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">So, instead of going through each of those potential solutions sequentially, we can compute the scores of all solutions at the same time.</p><p name="e0a0" id="e0a0" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">With this particular example, in theory, your quantum computer would be able to find one of the best solutions in a few milliseconds. Again, that’s 001 or 110 as we saw earlier:</p><p name="acc5" id="acc5" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">A | B | C | Score<br>0 | 0 | 0 | -1<br><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">0 | 0 | 1 | 1 &lt;- one of the best solutions</strong><br>0 | 1 | 0 | -1<br>0 | 1 | 1 | -1<br>1 | 0 | 0 | -1<br>1 | 0 | 1 | -1<br><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">1 | 1 | 0 | 1 &lt;- the other best solution</strong><br>1 | 1 | 1 | -1</p><p name="313d" id="313d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">In reality, to solve this problem, you would need to give your quantum computer two things:</p><ul class="postList"><li name="7633" id="7633" class="graf graf--li graf-after--p">All potential solutions represented with qubits</li><li name="3b49" id="3b49" class="graf graf--li graf-after--li">A function that turns each potential solution into a score. In this case, this is the function that counts the numbers of friend pairs and enemy pairs sharing the same car.</li></ul><p name="2d6d" id="2d6d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--li">Given these two things, your quantum computer will spit out one of the best solutions in a few milliseconds. In this case, that’s 001 or 110 with a score of 1.</p><p name="39c6" id="39c6" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Now, in theory, a quantum computer is able to find one of the best solutions every time it runs.</p><p name="e9c4" id="e9c4" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">However, in reality, there are errors when running a quantum computer. So, instead of finding the best solution, it might find the second-best solution, the third best solution, and so on.</p><p name="aa4c" id="aa4c" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">These errors become more prominent as the problem becomes more and more complex.</p><p name="1659" id="1659" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">So, in practice, you will probably want to run the same operation on a quantum computer dozens of times or hundreds of times. Then pick the best result out of the many results you get.</p><h4 name="f901" id="f901" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--p">How a quantum computer scales</h4><p name="f976" id="f976" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">Even with the errors I mentioned, the quantum computer does not have the same scaling issue a regular computer suffers from.</p><p name="abd7" id="abd7" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">When there are 3 people we need to divide into two cars, the number of operations we need to perform on a quantum computer is 1. This is because a quantum computer computes the score of all configurations at the same time.</p><p name="9063" id="9063" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">When there are 4 people, the number of operations is still 1.</p><p name="05d0" id="05d0" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">When there are 100 people, the number of operations is still 1. With a single operation, a quantum computer computes the scores of all <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">2¹⁰⁰</strong> ~= <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">10³⁰</strong> = <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">one million million million million million</strong> configurations at the same time.</p><p name="2299" id="2299" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">As I mentioned earlier, in practice, it’s probably best to run your quantum computer dozens of times or hundreds of times and pick the best result out of the many results you get.</p><p name="e57d" id="e57d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">However, it’s still much better than running the same problem on a regular computer and having to repeat the same type of computation one million million million million million times.</p><h4 name="c121" id="c121" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--p">Wrapping up</h4><p name="cbdd" id="cbdd" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4">Special thanks to everyone at D-Wave Systems for patiently explaining all of this to me.</p><p name="dfc4" id="dfc4" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">D-Wave recently launched a cloud environment for interacting with a quantum computer.</p><p name="dc9c" id="dc9c" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">If you’re a developer and would like actually to try using a quantum computer, it’s probably the easiest way to do so.</p><p name="1db3" id="1db3" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">It’s called Leap, and it’s at <a href="https://cloud.dwavesys.com/leap" data-href="https://cloud.dwavesys.com/leap" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">https://cloud.dwavesys.com/leap</a>.
